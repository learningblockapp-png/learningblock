The UK Wants to Ban Social Media for Under 16s. What Does That Mean for Parents?

If you've been anywhere near the news lately, you've probably seen the headlines. The UK government is in the middle of a proper tug of war over whether to ban social media for children under 16. The Children's Wellbeing and Schools Bill has been bouncing between the House of Commons and the House of Lords like a ping pong ball since January, and as of mid-April 2026, it's still going back and forth.

So what's actually happening?

The short version

In January 2026, the House of Lords voted to add a full social media ban for under 16s to the Children's Wellbeing and Schools Bill. The House of Commons voted it down. The Lords put it back. The Commons took it out again and proposed something slightly different: giving the government the power to make rules that would force social media companies to restrict certain features for children, rather than an outright ban.

At the same time, the government launched a public consultation in March 2026 asking for views on children's social media use, including whether there should be a minimum age. That consultation closes on 26 May 2026, with a response expected over the summer.

Australia already went ahead with a ban for under 16s back in December 2025, and the UK has been watching closely to see how that plays out.

Why it matters

Whatever side of the debate you land on, one thing is clear: the conversation about children and screens has shifted. It's no longer "should we do something?" but "what should we do?"

Research keeps piling up. A 2025 study found that children who use screens for two or more hours a day are 51% more likely to develop concentration difficulties. The Mental Health Foundation has pointed to links between excessive social media use and increased risk of depression and anxiety in young people. And algorithmically driven content that promotes self-harm and eating disorders has pushed this from a "nice to discuss" topic to an urgent one.

But the other side has a point too. Some young people rely on online communities for support, particularly those from marginalised groups. A blanket ban could push kids onto less regulated platforms where the risks might actually be worse. There's even a petition with nearly 64,000 signatures asking the government not to ban social media for under 16s.

What can parents actually do right now?

Here's the thing: whether or not a ban becomes law, it won't solve the day to day reality of managing your child's screen time. Legislation moves slowly. Your child's iPad does not.

What parents can do right now is take practical steps at home. That means setting clear boundaries around when and how long children use devices, choosing what they spend their screen time on, and making sure there's a balance between passive scrolling and something that's actually good for their brain.

Where this is heading

The consultation closes in May and the bill is still in play. By summer 2026 we'll have a much clearer picture of what the government plans to do. We'll keep this post updated as things develop.

In the meantime, the best thing you can do as a parent is not wait for legislation to solve the problem. Set the rules in your own home, make screen time something that's earned rather than expected, and give your child a reason to pick up some maths before they pick up YouTube.

That's a win regardless of what Parliament decides.
